# Electrical Terminology

| **Term**                                    | **Description**                                                                                                      |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Voltage (V)**                             | The electric potential difference between two points, pushing the current through a circuit.                         |
| **Current (I)**                             | The flow of electric charge, measured in amperes (A).                                                                |
| **Resistance (R)**                          | The opposition to the flow of current, measured in ohms (Ω).                                                         |
| **Power (P)**                               | The rate at which electrical energy is consumed or produced, measured in watts (W).                                  |
| **Ohm's Law**                               | A fundamental equation stating that voltage is equal to current times resistance (V = I × R).                        |
| **Ampere (A)**                              | The unit of electric current, representing the flow of one coulomb per second.                                       |
| **Circuit**                                 | A closed loop that allows electric current to flow, consisting of power sources, conductors, and loads.              |
| **Conductor**                               | A material that allows electricity to flow through it, usually a metal like copper or aluminum.                      |
| **Insulator**                               | A material that does not allow electricity to flow through it, such as rubber or plastic.                            |
| **Grounding**                               | The process of connecting electrical systems to the earth to ensure safety in case of a fault.                       |
| **Neutral Wire**                            | A wire that carries the return current back to the electrical panel, completing the circuit.                         |
| **Live (Hot) Wire**                         | The wire that carries the current from the power source to the load (usually black or red).                          |
| **Fuse**                                    | A safety device that protects electrical circuits by breaking the circuit if the current exceeds a certain level.    |
| **Circuit Breaker**                         | A switch that automatically opens a circuit when an overload or short circuit occurs, preventing damage.             |
| **GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)** | A safety device that detects ground faults and cuts off power to prevent electrical shocks.                          |
| **RCD (Residual Current Device)**           | A device that quickly disconnects the supply of electricity if it detects a leakage current to ground.               |
| **Transformer**                             | A device that changes the voltage of an alternating current (AC) in a circuit.                                       |
| **Watt (W)**                                | The unit of power in an electrical circuit, equal to one joule per second.                                           |
| **Induction**                               | The process of generating electric current in a conductor by changing magnetic fields.                               |
| **Capacitance**                             | The ability of a system to store electrical charge, measured in farads (F).                                          |
| **Inductor**                                | A component that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it.                                    |
| **Short Circuit**                           | A fault that occurs when a live wire comes in contact with a neutral or ground wire, creating a low-resistance path. |
| **Overload**                                | A condition where the current exceeds the rating of the circuit, causing a potential fire hazard.                    |
| **Conduit**                                 | A tube used to protect electrical wiring and keep it organized.                                                      |
| **Ground Fault**                            | An unintended path that allows current to flow from a live wire to the ground or other conductive surface.           |
| **Load**                                    | Any device or appliance that consumes electrical power in a circuit.                                                 |
| **Switch**                                  | A device used to open or close a circuit, controlling the flow of electricity.                                       |
| **Soldering**                               | The process of joining metal parts together by melting a filler metal.                                               |
| **Wire Gauge**                              | A measure of the thickness of a wire, determining how much current it can safely carry.                              |
| **Pigtail**                                 | A short length of wire used to connect one or more wires to a terminal or device.                                    |
| **Terminal Block**                          | A connector used to join electrical wires together in a safe and organized manner.                                   |
| **Busbar**                                  | A conductive strip or bar used to distribute electricity in a panel or distribution board.                           |
| **Panel Board**                             | The central control point for electrical circuits, housing the circuit breakers.                                     |
| **Surge Protector**                         | A device that protects electrical devices from voltage spikes by redirecting excess current.                         |
| **Electric Meter**                          | A device used to measure electrical energy consumption in a building or facility.                                    |
| **Earthing**                                | The process of connecting electrical devices or systems to the earth to ensure safety.                               |
| **Phase**                                   | A single cycle of an alternating current (AC) signal, typically with three phases in commercial power.               |
| **Wiring Diagram**                          | A schematic representation of an electrical system, showing the components and their connections.                    |
