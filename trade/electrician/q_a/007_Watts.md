# Watts: The Unit of Power

**Watts (W)** is the **SI unit of power** and is used to measure the rate at which electrical energy is used or produced
in a circuit. Power is defined as the **rate at which work is done** or energy is transferred.

### **Definition**:

1 **watt** is equal to **1 joule per second**. This means that a device using **1 watt of power** consumes **1 joule of
energy** every second.

\[
P = V \times I
\]

Where:

- **P** = Power (Watts)
- **V** = Voltage (Volts)
- **I** = Current (Amperes)

### **What Does Watts Measure?**

- Watts measure the **rate of energy consumption** or **production** in an electrical system. It tells us how much
  electrical energy is being used by a device in a given time frame.
- The power consumed by an electrical device is determined by the **voltage** applied to it and the **current** flowing
  through it.

### **Units of Power**:

- The **watt (W)** is the basic unit of power. For higher amounts of power, we use multiples of watts:
    - **Kilowatt (kW)** = 1,000 watts
    - **Megawatt (MW)** = 1,000,000 watts
    - **Gigawatt (GW)** = 1,000,000,000 watts

### **Power Calculation**:

Power in an electrical circuit can be calculated by multiplying **voltage** and **current**.

\[
P = V \times I
\]

Where:

- **P** is the power in watts (W),
- **V** is the voltage in volts (V),
- **I** is the current in amperes (A).

### **Example of Watts in Real Life**:

- **Light Bulb**: A typical **LED light bulb** might consume **10 watts** of power. This means it uses 10 joules of
  energy every second.
- **Home Appliances**: A **refrigerator** might use **100 watts**, while a **microwave** could use around **1,000 watts
  ** (1 kW).

### **Calculating Energy Usage**:

If a device uses **1 watt** of power for **1 hour**, the energy consumed would be:

\[
E = P \times t = 1 \, W \times 1 \, h = 1 \, Wh
\]

Where:

- **E** is energy in watt-hours (Wh),
- **P** is power in watts (W),
- **t** is time in hours (h).

For a **1,000-watt (1 kW)** appliance running for **2 hours**, the energy consumed would be:

\[
E = 1,000 \, W \times 2 \, h = 2,000 \, Wh = 2 \, kWh
\]

This calculation helps to understand **energy consumption** in terms of **kilowatt-hours (kWh)**, which is what your
electricity bill is often based on.
